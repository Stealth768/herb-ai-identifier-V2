Amla Nutritional Value and Powerful Antioxidant Benefits
Amla is far more than an ordinary fruit. It contains an impressive mix of essential nutrients that support overall health in natural ways. One of its most striking qualities is its extremely high vitamin C content, often described as many times higher than that of oranges. According to the Indian Council of Medical Research (ICMR) - National Institute of Nutrition (NIN), 100g of amla contains approximately 600mg of Vitamin C, which is nearly 20 times the concentration found in orange juice. Along with vitamin C, amla provides polyphenols and plant compounds that make it a strong natural antioxidant fruit, helping protect the body from harmful cell damage.

It also delivers key minerals such as calcium, phosphorus, and iron, along with vitamins A and the B-complex group. Together, these nutrients support immunity, energy, vision, and tissue repair. Because of this balanced nutrition, the benefits of amla are widely respected in both traditional wellness and modern health discussions.

The antioxidant strength of amla in Ayurveda deserves special attention. Research published indicates that amla possesses one of the highest ORAC (Oxygen Radical Absorbance Capacity) values among fruits, recorded at 261,500 μmol TE/100g. Antioxidants protect the body from oxidative stress, which contributes to aging and long-term illness. Amla’s high ORAC value shows its strong ability to neutralize free radicals effectively.

This powerful nutritional profile explains why Ayurvedic benefits of amla are considered supportive for long-term vitality rather than short-term relief.

Side Effects, Dosage, and Who Should Avoid Amla
Amla is generally safe, but excessive intake may cause digestive discomfort in some individuals. Starting with a small amount helps the body adjust comfortably.

There is no universal amla dosage, as needs vary by age, health status, and purpose. General health guidelines from the National Health Portal of India suggest a daily intake of 1–2 fresh amla fruits or 10–20 ml of fresh juice to meet the recommended dietary allowance (RDA). Professional medical advice helps determine the right intake.

Some people may need caution, especially those with certain medical conditions or medication use. Understanding side effects of amla and knowing who should avoid amla ensures safe and balanced consumption.
